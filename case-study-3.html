Content Strategy & Education

Educational Storytelling for Social Impact at MIT Solve

How I designed a content strategy from scratch to educate diverse audiences about global innovators — reaching 168,000+ interactions through research-driven storytelling.

Role

Marketing Intelligence Specialist

Timeline

Jul – Dec 2023

Audience

General public, social innovators, donors & stakeholders

Frameworks

Audience Analysis, Bloom's Taxonomy, Content Scaffolding, A/B Testing

Organization

MIT Solve

The Challenge

MIT Solve connects social entrepreneurs with the resources they need to drive global change — from education access in prisons to affordable autism therapy to assistive technology for the visually impaired. But the innovators and their work were complex, technical, and deeply contextual. The challenge was making their stories accessible and compelling to three very different audiences simultaneously.

There was no existing content framework. No templates, no editorial guidelines, no established voice. I was building the entire content strategy from scratch — figuring out how to tell stories that would educate the general public, inspire fellow innovators, and give donors confidence that their investment was creating real impact.

Key Challenges

  • Three distinct audiences with different knowledge levels, motivations, and needs
  • Complex social impact work that needed to be made accessible without oversimplifying
  • No existing content framework, templates, or editorial guidelines — building from zero
  • Content needed to both educate and drive measurable engagement across 5 social platforms
  • 3+ articles per month, each requiring deep research and founder interviews

My Approach

01

Audience Analysis & Content Mapping

I started by mapping the three audience segments and their distinct needs. The general public needed context and emotional connection — they didn't know what assistive technology for the visually impaired even looked like. Innovators needed validation and visibility — proof that their work mattered. Donors needed evidence of impact — concrete outcomes that justified continued investment.

Using this analysis, I designed a content structure that layered information: lead with the human story (for everyone), build in the technical innovation (for innovators), and close with measurable impact (for donors). One article, three audiences served.

02

Research-Driven Storytelling Framework

Each article required deep research and a founder interview. I developed a repeatable interview framework that extracted the right information for all three audience layers: the personal origin story, the technical approach, and the impact data. This framework ensured consistency across 3+ articles per month while allowing each story to feel unique and authentic.

03

Scaffolded Content Design

I applied instructional scaffolding principles to the article structure. Complex topics like prison education technology or AI-powered Braille displays were introduced through the founder's personal journey first — building empathy and context before introducing technical concepts. This mirrors Gagné's approach of gaining attention and stimulating recall before presenting new material.

For example, the article on UnlockED didn't start with "learning management systems in correctional facilities." It started with Jessica Hicklin being sentenced to life at age 16 and teaching herself to code without internet access. By the time readers reached the technical details, they cared.

04

Multi-Channel Distribution & Optimization

I built an analytics framework across 5 social platforms, tracking 50+ metrics to understand what content resonated with which audience. I ran A/B tests on headlines, formats, and distribution timing — then used the data to iterate on the content strategy. This wasn't just publishing; it was a continuous feedback loop between content design and audience response.

Design Decisions

Human Story First

Every article opened with the founder's personal journey — not the product or the data. This created an emotional entry point that worked for all three audiences, regardless of their technical background.

One Article, Three Layers

Rather than creating separate content for each audience, I designed a layered structure: narrative hook (public), technical depth (innovators), impact evidence (donors). Readers naturally self-selected the depth they needed.

Repeatable Interview Framework

I built a structured interview guide that consistently extracted origin story, innovation details, and impact metrics from every founder. This ensured quality and consistency at scale — 3+ articles per month without sacrificing depth.

Data-Informed Iteration

Content wasn't a one-way broadcast. The analytics framework I built turned every article into a learning opportunity — what headlines worked, which stories drove donor engagement, where readers dropped off. Each insight fed back into the next piece.

Reflection

This project shaped how I think about learning design at its most fundamental level. Every article was essentially a micro-learning experience — taking someone from "I know nothing about prison education technology" to "I understand why this matters and how it works" in a 5-minute read. The principles are the same whether you're designing a training module or a piece of content: know your audience, scaffold the complexity, lead with relevance, and measure what works.

Working at MIT Solve also deepened my commitment to accessible design. Writing about innovators building assistive technology for the visually impaired, education tools for incarcerated learners, and therapy kits for children with autism reinforced that good design meets people where they are — not where we assume they should be.
