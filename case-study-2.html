Curriculum Design

Making Ghalib Accessible: Teaching Urdu Poetry to Global Learners

How I designed a multimedia learning experience to help 45 undergrads with no South Asian literary background engage deeply with one of Urdu's greatest poets.

Role

Graduate Teaching Fellow

Timeline

2023 – 2024

Audience

45 undergraduates, Global Literature course

Frameworks

Bloom's Taxonomy, Gagné's Nine Events, Adult Learning Theory

Institution

Northeastern University

The Challenge

As part of an undergraduate Global Literature course at Northeastern University, I was responsible for teaching a unit on the poetry of Mirza Ghalib — widely regarded as one of the greatest Urdu and Persian poets. The challenge was significant: a class of 45 students, almost none of whom had any background in South Asian literature, Urdu language, or the Mughal-era cultural context that shaped Ghalib's work.

The existing course materials treated Ghalib the way most Western syllabi treat non-Western poets — translated excerpts with minimal context. Students were expected to analyze poetry that was deeply rooted in a linguistic and cultural tradition they had no frame of reference for. The result was predictable: low engagement, surface-level analysis, and a sense that the material was inaccessible.

Key Challenges

  • 45 students with no prior exposure to Urdu poetry or South Asian literary traditions
  • Poetry that relies heavily on linguistic nuance, wordplay, and cultural context that doesn't survive direct translation
  • Existing materials were text-only translated excerpts with minimal scaffolding
  • Risk of students disengaging from material that felt culturally distant

My Approach

01

Learner Analysis & Gap Identification

I started by assessing what students already knew and what they'd need to engage meaningfully with the material. Using Bloom's Taxonomy, I identified that the course expected analysis-level thinking (interpreting metaphor, evaluating poetic technique) but students lacked the foundational knowledge layer — they didn't have the historical, cultural, or linguistic context to even begin interpreting.

02

Building the Context Layer

Before touching the poetry itself, I designed a multimedia presentation that immersed students in Ghalib's world. I curated historical photographs of Mughal-era Delhi, sourced original documentary footage, and mapped out the political and personal circumstances that shaped each poem. The goal was to make students feel the weight of the context before they encountered the words.

03

Multimedia-First Delivery

Rather than starting with text on a page, I had students listen to Ghalib's poetry performed in the original Urdu — through recordings and documentary clips. Even without understanding the language, hearing the rhythm, tone, and musicality gave them an emotional entry point. I then layered in the English translations alongside the historical context, so students could connect sound, meaning, and story.

04

Scaffolded Discussion & Analysis

I designed discussion prompts that moved students up Bloom's levels progressively: from recall ("What was happening in Delhi when this was written?") to analysis ("How does the historical context change your reading of this couplet?") to evaluation ("How does Ghalib's approach to loss compare to the Western poets we've studied?"). This gave students a structured path from unfamiliarity to genuine literary engagement.

Design Decisions

Sound Before Text

Hearing the poetry in Urdu — even without comprehension — created an emotional connection that reading a translation alone never could. This follows Gagné's first event: gaining attention through something unexpected and sensory.

Context as Scaffolding

Instead of footnotes, I built the historical and cultural context into the lesson flow as a prerequisite layer. Students understood the "why" before the "what," which is a core adult learning principle — relevance drives engagement.

Visual Storytelling

Historical photographs and documentary footage made an abstract cultural context tangible. Seeing the streets of 19th-century Delhi gave students a mental model to anchor the poetry to — turning distant history into something they could picture.

Comparative Analysis

Connecting Ghalib to Western poets students already knew gave them a bridge. Rather than treating Urdu poetry as "other," I positioned it within a shared human tradition of writing about love, loss, and meaning.

Outcomes & Impact

45

Students engaged with Urdu poetry for the first time

1 yr

Curriculum used across multiple semesters

3+

Media types integrated (audio, video, visual, text)

What Worked

  • Students who initially found the material intimidating became genuinely curious — several sought out more Ghalib on their own
  • Discussion quality shifted from surface-level paraphrasing to comparative literary analysis
  • The multimedia approach became a model for how other non-Western poetry units could be taught in the course
  • Demonstrated that "inaccessible" content is usually a design problem, not a learner problem

Reflection

This project taught me that the biggest barrier to learning isn't complexity — it's missing context. Ghalib's poetry isn't inherently harder than Shakespeare; it just requires a different set of scaffolding. The learning designer's job is to build that bridge, not to simplify the destination.

If I were to iterate, I'd add a pre-assessment to gauge students' baseline familiarity with South Asian culture, and I'd build in a reflective journaling component where students track how their understanding evolves across the unit. I'd also explore building an interactive Articulate Rise module that students could revisit outside of class — pairing the audio recordings with annotated translations and contextual timelines.
